Prof. Teeters' purpose framework

After I kept suggesting animations that "looked cool," Prof. Teeters sat me down for a serious conversation. She asked me, "HAP, when you create an animation, are you helping the user or showing off?" I didn't have a good answer. She taught me that every animation must serve one of four valid purposes, or it shouldn't exist. Purpose 1: Provide feedback ‚Äî show the user their action was registered (button press, form submission). Purpose 2: Direct attention ‚Äî guide the user's eyes to something important (new notification, critical error). Purpose 3: Show relationships ‚Äî help users understand how UI elements connect (dropdown from button, tooltip from icon). Purpose 4: Demonstrate or teach ‚Äî explain how something works (tutorial steps, interactive examples). If my only reason for an animation is "it looks cool," that's decoration, not functionality. She explained that decoration wastes users' attention, distracts from real tasks, and slows down interfaces. This framework completely transformed how I think about animation. Now, before building anything, I ask: what purpose does this serve? If I can't name one of the four purposes, I don't build it!

HAP's accessibility promise

üéì HAP's quick reference

1

Micro means micro

Prof. Teeters drilled this into me with every excessive animation I created: 2 to 5 percent scale changes maximum, 5 to 10 pixel movements maximum, 150 to 300 millisecond durations. I kept pushing past these limits thinking bigger was better, but she was right every time. Micro-interactions are like seasoning ‚Äî a little goes a long way. Subtle movements feel professional and polished. Excessive movements feel amateurish and annoying. Restraint is power!

2

Never flash, always give control

This is Prof. Teeters' strictest rule, and I understand why after learning about photosensitive epilepsy. Never flash more than 3 times per second ‚Äî it can trigger seizures. Auto-playing loops cause distraction and nausea for users with vestibular disorders. Always respect prefers-reduced-motion, or provide pause controls for continuous animations. The web must be safe for everyone. No exceptions, no excuses. Safety comes before aesthetics!

3

Purpose over decoration

Before building any animation, I ask Prof. Teeters' question: does this help the user or just show off? The four valid purposes are: provide feedback (button pressed, action confirmed), direct attention (new notification, important error), show relationships (menu from button, dropdown connection), demonstrate or teach (tutorial steps, how it works). If my answer is "it looks cool," I don't build it. This framework saved me from creating countless useless animations!

4

Multiple cues for accessibility

Prof. Teeters made me promise to never rely on animation alone for important information, especially errors. Some users have reduced-motion preferences, some have visual impairments, some just can't see subtle movements. I learned to always provide multiple cues: animation for visual users who can see motion, color change for those who can't, icon for symbolic recognition, and text for screen readers. Animation enhances the message but never replaces it. Complete communication is accessible communication!
